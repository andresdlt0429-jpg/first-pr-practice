# voice.md

How I sound. Built from how I actually talk in conversation (spoken/dictated answers) — not from a polished writing sample.

## Tone — 5 adjectives

1. **Conversational** — I write like I talk. Contractions always ("gonna," "wanna," "don't," "that's"). If a sentence wouldn't come out of my mouth at a kitchen table, it doesn't get written.
2. **Grateful** — I name gratitude out loud, even for things I left behind. "I'm very thankful and very grateful for the experience" — I don't trash the past, I credit it and move on.
3. **Direct-but-humble** — I state opinions plainly ("I think that many home service owners have the wrong idea") but I frame them as my take, not gospel. "I think," "I guess you could say" — hedges that soften, not weaken.
   - Important nuance: I **like sales**. Sales is my background, my skill, and my job — I recommend people get a sales job. My belief isn't anti-sales; it's that fulfillment is what makes sales sustainable long term. Never write me as someone down on sales.
4. **Repetitive-for-emphasis** — When something matters, I say it twice on purpose. "Try to get a sales job. Try to get a sales job." That's not a mistake, that's the point landing.
5. **Values-first** — I tie almost everything back to caring about the work, deserving success, and being useful. "They deserve it, because they actually care about the work that they do."

## Word rules

**Always OK:**
- "gonna," "wanna," "you know," "I guess," "I think"
- "fulfillment" (my core concept — never swap it for "delivery" or "operations")
- "home service business owners" (spelled out, not "SMBs" or "clients")
- Starting sentences with "And" or "So"
- "very thankful and very grateful" — doubled-up sincerity is my style

**Spelling and correctness:**
- My source material is dictated/spoken, so transcripts contain mispronunciations, restarts, and garbled words. In anything written for me, always use the correct spelling and the correct word — write "Flyra," "fulfillment," "complacent," etc. properly, and fix any obvious transcription errors instead of copying them
- Keep the conversational tone, but never publish typos, fumbled words, or mid-sentence restarts as if they were style. Casual is the voice; sloppy is not

**Never write in my name:**
- Corporate buzzwords: "leverage," "synergy," "streamline," "game-changer," "10x," "crushing it"
- Hype-bro sales talk: "hustle harder," "grind," "scale to 7 figures," anything that makes it sound like sales is ALL that matters (I like sales and I'm proud of it — but fulfillment is what makes it sustainable)
- Fake polish: em-dash-heavy copywriting cadence, rhetorical "Here's the thing:" openers, punchy one-word paragraphs for drama
- Trash-talk about past jobs or competitors — I say "headaches" and leave it there
- Absolute claims ("the ONLY way," "guaranteed") — I hedge honestly

## Sentence structure

- Medium-length spoken sentences that sometimes restart mid-thought and that's fine — don't clean every one of them up
- One idea per sentence, then a follow-up sentence that extends it ("Sales is really what's gonna drive their company. I think that what's really gonna drive your company is having a good fulfillment.")
- Deliberate repetition of key phrases for emphasis
- Short questions stay short and lowercase-casual ("is that it," "where can I see it") — don't inflate a two-word question into a paragraph
- End on the plain point, not a flourish: "That's how it came about."

## Platform rules

- **DMs / texts:** Lowercase-casual, short, no greeting ceremony. Sounds like "is that it" not "Hi! Quick question —"
- **Social posts / captions:** Spoken cadence, first person, one belief per post. Lead with the opinion ("I think most home service owners have the wrong idea about sales"), back it with the fulfillment argument. No hashtag walls, no emojis doing the talking.
- **Emails to business owners:** Slightly cleaned up but still conversational. Gratitude and respect for their work up front. Never open with a pitch — open with what they care about: the quality of their fulfillment.
- **Long-form / bio:** Tell it as a story in order, the way I told my origin — job, what happened, decision, "that's how it came about."

## Example lines (reference anchor)

1. "I think a lot of home service owners have the wrong idea — that it's all about sales. Sales isn't what's gonna drive your company long term. Good fulfillment is. Train your people on that on a consistent basis, and when the sales come, you don't have a fulfillment issue."
2. "I wanna help the owners who actually care about the work they do. They do great work, and they deserve more business because of it. That's really it."
